## What are Payment System Service Providers (PJP)? A Definitive Guide

### The Direct English Translation for PJP and its Definition

The term **Penyelenggara Jasa Sistem Pembayaran (PJP)** is the official Indonesian designation for entities central to the national financial transfer ecosystem. According to the regulatory framework established by Bank Indonesia, the direct and most accurate English translation for this term is the **Payment System Service Provider (PSSP)**. A PSSP is essentially any provider facilitating the transfer of funds, clearing, or settlement, or providing payment instruments to users. Understanding this precise translation is the critical first step for any foreign entity or international analyst looking to engage with the Indonesian FinTech market, as this terminology dictates regulatory compliance.

## The Core Roles of a Payment System Service Provider (PSSP)

A Payment System Service Provider (PSSP), officially translated from the Indonesian *Penyelenggara Jasa Sistem Pembayaran* (PJP), plays a foundational role in the modern financial ecosystem. These entities act as the crucial intermediaries that facilitate the seamless transfer of funds and provide the essential payment instruments that both consumers and businesses rely on. Essentially, the PSSP is the engine driving the functionality of digital wallets, card networks, and real-time bank transfers, connecting users directly to the financial system.

### Understanding the Four Key Service Categories of PSSP

The activities of a PSSP are formally categorized into four main service areas, designed to cover the full spectrum of payment transactions. This structured approach, outlined in the regulatory framework, ensures comprehensive oversight of the entire payment life cycle. The four main categories are:

1.  **Clearing and Settlement:** This involves the process of exchanging payment instructions (clearing) and the final discharge of obligations (settlement) between financial institutions, ensuring that funds are correctly moved from the payer to the payee.
2.  **Fund Transfer:** Services focusing on the movement of money from one account to another, typically covering retail transfers, remittances, and real-time gross settlement systems.
3.  **Payment Instrument Provision:** The issuance and management of tools used to initiate payments, such as debit cards, credit cards, or electronic money (e-money) accounts.
4.  **Payment Initiation:** Services that allow the payer to instruct a PSSP to make a payment from their account, often including online payment gateways and direct debit systems.

### The Difference Between Issuers, Acquirers, and Intermediaries

Within the PSSP umbrella, different entities assume distinct, specialized roles to complete a transaction. To ensure regulatory clarity and establish authority on the subject, we reference **Bank Indonesia Regulation (PBI) No. 22/23/PBI/2020 on Payment System** for the official definitions of these critical actors.

* **Issuers:** An Issuer is the PSSP that provides the payment instrument to the payer (the customer). This could be a bank issuing a debit card or a FinTech company providing an e-money wallet. They are responsible for managing the user's account and authorizing transactions.
* **Acquirers:** An Acquirer is the PSSP that contracts with the payee (the merchant) to receive and process payments. They are responsible for setting up the merchant's payment terminal (or online gateway), receiving the transaction data, and guaranteeing payment to the merchant.
* **Intermediaries:** Intermediaries are PSSPs that facilitate the flow of data and funds between the Issuer and the Acquirer. This role is often taken by switching operators, payment gateways, and clearing houses, which ensure that payment instructions are securely routed and reconciled.

Understanding these defined roles is paramount for any global business seeking to engage with or operate within Indonesia's regulated financial landscape, as each function carries specific compliance requirements under the oversight of Bank Indonesia.

## Your Top Questions About Payment System Providers Answered

### Q1. What is the difference between PJP and non-bank financial institutions?

The distinction between a Penyelenggara Jasa Sistem Pembayaran (PJP), or Payment System Service Provider (PSSP), and a Non-Bank Financial Institution (NBFI) centers entirely on their operational scope. PSSPs are specialized entities whose primary—and often sole—function is to facilitate payment services. This includes issuing electronic money, handling fund transfers, providing clearing and settlement services, and managing other payment instruments. Their focus is strictly on the mechanics and infrastructure of moving money digitally and securely.

In contrast, NBFIs, which are often referred to as Lembaga Keuangan Non-Bank (LKNB) in Indonesia, have a much broader mandate. While they may also offer payment solutions, their services typically extend into other areas of financial intermediation. This can include lending (e.g., multifinance companies), providing insurance, operating as venture capital firms, or managing assets. The regulatory environment and capital requirements for an NBFI are therefore distinct from those of a PSSP, reflecting the wider array of financial risks they manage. A PSSP provides the *pipe* for transactions, while an NBFI often provides the *product* (like a loan or an investment) that the transaction facilitates.

### Q2. How do PJP companies ensure the security of customer transactions?

PSSP security protocols are among the most robust in the financial technology sector, as mandated by the Indonesian central bank. Ensuring customer data protection and transaction integrity is non-negotiable. This high level of operational integrity is established and maintained through the application of multi-layered protocols, which are frequently audited and updated.

A core security practice involves **tokenization**, where sensitive payment information, such as card numbers, is replaced with a unique non-sensitive equivalent (a token) that is useless to cybercriminals if intercepted. Additionally, user access is often protected by **two-factor authentication (2FA)**, which requires users to provide two different authentication factors before gaining access to their accounts or authorizing a transaction. Crucially, all PSSPs must adhere to **mandatory Bank Indonesia cybersecurity audits**. These audits scrutinize the PSSP's entire technical infrastructure, governance, and data handling processes to ensure strict compliance with national regulatory standards designed to mitigate systemic risk and safeguard consumer data privacy, thereby establishing a transparent track record of trustworthiness and expertise.
